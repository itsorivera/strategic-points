# The ONC Framework: Strategic Architecture for Meetings

_Optimizing Group Interactions through the Open-Narrow-Close Technique_

## The Triple-Phase Interaction Model

Every effective conversation or meeting requires a clear architectural arc. The Open-Narrow-Close (ONC) framework provides a roadmap for guiding a group from initial exploration to decisive commitment.

---

## 1. Opening: Broadening the Perspective

The opening phase is designed to surface ideas, introduce core issues, and stimulate creative thought.

- **Tactical Momentum:** Move through introductions and agenda-sharing rapidly. Audiences often become frustrated if the transition to "real content" is delayed.
- **High-Energy Ideation:** Instead of extended brainstorming sessions, utilize short, high-stress bursts (e.g., a "3-minute clock"). This heightens energy and forces participants to "fire out" ideas without self-censorship.
- **Goal:** To establish a diverse and broad set of options or viewpoints.

---

## 2. Narrowing: The Critical Pivot

The narrowing phase is where the most advanced facilitative skill is required. This is the transition from "what is possible" to "what is priority."

- **The Decision Hardship:** Narrowing involves making difficult choices and filtering out ideas. Because participants have "skin in the game," this phase introduces social jeopardy and potential ego conflicts.
- **Process Fairness:** For participants to accept a conclusion, they must believe in the fairness and transparency of the selection process. Facilitators must provide ample time for this stage to ensure buy-in.
- **Goal:** To synthesize the broad opening ideas into a prioritized focus for the meeting's objectives.

---

## 3. Closing: Driving toward Commitment

The closing phase ensures that the interaction results in tangible progress and organizational alignment.

- **Restating for Alignment:** Clearly and explicitly restate every agreement reached during the narrowing phase. This ensures that everyone leaves with the same understanding of "what was agreed."
- **Actionable Takeaways:** Focus on commitments, next steps, and specific individual responsibilities.
- **Momentum Mode:** Maintain a high pace to avoid getting "stuck" in revisitng previous debates. Even in day-long workshops, the closing should be concise (20-30 minutes).
- **Goal:** To achieve a successful conclusion with clear, shared commitments.

---

## Strategic Time Allocation

Proportional time management is essential for a balanced meeting:

- **Opening:** Quick to start, moderate time for dynamic ideation.
- **Narrowing:** Requires the largest time allocation due to the complexity of decision-making and consensus-building.
- **Closing:** Fast-paced to maintain energy and focus on results.

_Transition timing depends on context: the importance of the ideas required, the significance of the specific project, and whether the interaction is an initial discovery or a final decision point._

---

## Origins and Theoretical Roots

The Open-Narrow-Close framework is a fundamental facilitation model used to manage group dynamics during the decision-making process.

- **Source:** The specific "Open-Narrow-Close" terminology and structure were prominently developed by the **Interaction Institute for Social Change (IISC)**. It is a core component of their _Facilitative Leadership_ methodology.
- **Theoretical Foundation:** The framework is built upon the concepts of **Divergent and Convergent thinking**. This approach was popularized in the field of facilitation by **Sam Kaner** and his "Diamond of Participatory Decision-Making."
- **Industry Adoption:** While its origins lie in social change and facilitative leadership, the model has been widely adopted by elite management consultancies—such as **McKinsey & Company**—as a strategic tool for ensuring meetings drive toward alignment and action rather than aimless discussion.
